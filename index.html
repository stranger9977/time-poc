They see the future.

Four exhibits arguing that “time slows down” for great athletes because of anticipation — built on 2023–24 MLB pitch tracking, Olympic start-block sensors, and a formalized version of Michael's commit-window sketch.

Exhibit 1 · The impossible deadline

A fastball leaves you ~150 ms to decide.

Light needs ~100 ms to travel from a hitter's retina to a cortical decision, and the swing itself takes ~150 ms to execute. Both are fixed biology. Whatever is left in the middle is the decision window — and at elite velocity, it's a rounding error. Reaction alone can't hit a fastball. Prediction can.

Exhibit 2 · The aging experiment

The hitters with the slowest reflexes are the hardest to fool.

Raw reaction speed peaks in your early 20s and declines every year after. The cue library — anticipation — only grows. So if hitting a fastball were a reaction task, hitters pushing 40 should be the most fooled people in the sport, especially at top-end velocity where the decision window collapses toward zero. Exhibit 3 · The biological floor

Raw reaction hit a wall. Anticipation is the only door left.

World Athletics disqualifies any start under 100 ms on the grounds that no human can react that fast — it must have been a guess. The fastest humans alive, wired to force plates in the blocks, cluster at 0.11–0.18 s and haven't gotten faster in decades. Everything that separates elite from ordinary happens before the stimulus. (Noah Lyles had the slowest reaction in the Paris final. He won it.)

Exhibit 4 · The duel

Anticipation is a poker game. Great players commit late and leak nothing.

Everything so far treats the defender as a stopwatch. But the defense is running the same download → commit → react loop, and both sides are playing a second game on top: maximize the information you gather, minimize the information you reveal. The offense's weapon is possibility — every live option they carry into the commit is one more branch the defense must be ready for. That's not just a metaphor: Hick's law says reaction time grows with each additional option a defender has to sort through. Disguise doesn't just hide the future. It slows the defender's clock.

Reading it: the defense's clock can't start until intent is readable — the offense's commit minus whatever cues leaked early. From there the defense pays a decide cost that grows with every option still alive (Hick's law), then a fixed close cost to actually move. Slide the commit later, seal the leaks, keep a counter alive — and the defense's window collapses below biology, exactly like the 98 mph fastball in Exhibit 2. And after the commit, the fan re-opens: the window repeats until the play is dead.

Model, not measurement: illustrative parameters — 180 ms base reaction (Exhibit 3's floor plus a choice premium), ~100 ms per bit of uncertainty (Hick–Hyman slope), 100 ms to close, deadline at 750 ms. The point is the structure: elite offense shrinks the defense's certainty window on one side (late commit, no leaks) and stretches the defense's required processing on the other (live options). Both moves buy time — or steal it.

Where this goes in the video: Exhibit 1 sets the impossible deadline, Exhibit 2 proves the edge is learned prediction (not youth), Exhibit 3 shows biology already maxed out reaction, and Exhibit 4 closes the loop — anticipation is adversarial, a duel over who reveals intent first, where possibility itself slows the opponent's clock. So the “time slows down” feeling is a prediction engine running ahead of the present. Bridges cleanly to the memory-density half (Eagleman's falling experiment: time doesn't slow live, it slows in the recording).

Sources & method

  • Pitch data: Baseball Savant / Statcast, 2023–24 regular seasons, four-seam fastballs. Flight time computed as (60.5 ft − release extension) ÷ average ball velocity, with the ball assumed to lose ~9.5% of release speed by the plate.
  • Visual latency (~100 ms to conscious visual processing) and bat swing execution (~150 ms) are standard sports-science figures used for illustration; exact values vary by player and study.
  • Whiff = swinging strike ÷ swings; hitter age = seasonal age (June 30). Sample sizes shown in tooltips and table view.
  • Reaction times: official World Athletics results — Paris 2024 final, Tokyo 2020 final. False-start rule: World Athletics TR16.7 (reaction < 100 ms).
  • ~273 ms median human click reaction: Human Benchmark aggregate data (visual stimulus + mouse click, so includes device latency; the Olympic figures are force-plate measurements — different instruments, same order of magnitude).
  • Exhibit 4 is a conceptual model of Michael's commit-window sketch, not a measurement. The decide-cost curve is the Hick–Hyman law (choice reaction time grows ~linearly with the log of the number of alternatives); parameter values are illustrative.
