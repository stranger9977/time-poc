Four exhibits arguing that “time slows down” for great athletes because of anticipation — built on 2023–24 MLB pitch tracking, Olympic start-block sensors, and a playable version of Michael's commit-window sketch.

Exhibit 1 · The impossible deadline

A fastball leaves you ~150 ms to decide.

Light needs ~100 ms to travel from a hitter's retina to a cortical decision, and the swing itself takes ~150 ms to execute. Both are fixed biology. Whatever is left in the middle is the decision window — and at elite velocity, it's a rounding error. Reaction alone can't hit a fastball. Prediction can.

Exhibit 2 · The aging experiment

The hitters with the slowest reflexes are the hardest to fool.

Raw reaction speed peaks in your early 20s and declines every year after. The cue library — anticipation — only grows. So if hitting a fastball were a reaction task, hitters pushing 40 should be the most fooled people in the sport, especially at top-end velocity where the decision window collapses toward zero. Exhibit 3 · The biological floor

Raw reaction hit a wall. Anticipation is the only door left.

World Athletics disqualifies any start under 100 ms on the grounds that no human can react that fast — it must have been a guess. The fastest humans alive, wired to force plates in the blocks, cluster at 0.11–0.18 s and haven't gotten faster in decades. Everything that separates elite from ordinary happens before the stimulus. (Noah Lyles had the slowest reaction in the Paris final. He won it.)

Exhibit 4 · The duel

Now you're the defender. Good luck.

The defense runs the same download → commit → react loop as the offense, and both sides play a second game on top: maximize the information you gather, minimize the information you reveal. The offense's weapon is possibility — and there's a century-old law of psychology that prices it. Hick's law: reaction time grows with every option you're forced to hold live. First the map, then the territory: step through the duel below, then get in a stance and defend it yourself.

Why this matters on a court: the defender's budget is the commit-to-arrival window — roughly the 600 ms line above. Your stage-1 self probably lives inside it. Your stage-3 self might not. Nothing about your body changed between stages; the offense added milliseconds to your clock by refusing to tell the future. That's what "shifty" is. Chris Paul's counters aren't just moves — they're time, installed in the defender's head. And the elite version of defense is refusing to pay full price: reading cues early (Exhibit 2) to collapse four options back to one before the commit ever happens.

Absolute times include your screen and input latency, so compare stages to each other, not to the Olympians above. Hick–Hyman: RT = a + b·log₂(N+1); slope b runs ~100–150 ms per bit for the unpracticed. Elite athletes drill the slope flatter — practice is another way of buying time.

Where this goes in the video: Exhibit 1 sets the impossible deadline, Exhibit 2 proves the edge is learned prediction (not youth), Exhibit 3 shows biology already maxed out reaction, and Exhibit 4 closes the loop by making you play it — anticipation is adversarial, and every option the offense keeps alive measurably stretches the defender's clock. So the “time slows down” feeling is a prediction engine running ahead of the present. Bridges cleanly to the memory-density half (Eagleman's falling experiment: time doesn't slow live, it slows in the recording).

Sources & method

  • Pitch data: Baseball Savant / Statcast, 2023–24 regular seasons, four-seam fastballs. Flight time computed as (60.5 ft − release extension) ÷ average ball velocity, with the ball assumed to lose ~9.5% of release speed by the plate.
  • Visual latency (~100 ms to conscious visual processing) and bat swing execution (~150 ms) are standard sports-science figures used for illustration; exact values vary by player and study.
  • Whiff = swinging strike ÷ swings; hitter age = seasonal age (June 30). Sample sizes shown in tooltips and table view.
  • Reaction times: official World Athletics results — Paris 2024 final, Tokyo 2020 final. False-start rule: World Athletics TR16.7 (reaction < 100 ms).
  • ~273 ms median human click reaction: Human Benchmark aggregate data (visual stimulus + mouse click, so includes device latency; the Olympic figures are force-plate measurements — different instruments, same order of magnitude).
  • Exhibit 4 measures your own choice reaction times in-browser (device latency included — compare stages to each other). The straight-line prediction is the Hick–Hyman law: RT = a + b·log₂(N+1), with slope b typically ~100–150 ms/bit untrained.
