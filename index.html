Three exhibits arguing that “time slows down” for great athletes because of anticipation — built on 2023–24 MLB pitch tracking and Olympic start-block sensors.

Exhibit 1 · The impossible deadline

A fastball leaves you ~150 ms to decide.

Light needs ~100 ms to travel from a hitter's retina to a cortical decision, and the swing itself takes ~150 ms to execute. Both are fixed biology. Whatever is left in the middle is the decision window — and at elite velocity, it's a rounding error. Reaction alone can't hit a fastball. Prediction can.

Read it like a camera: by the time the ball is halfway home, the swing decision is already locked. The only way to buy more time is to start the computation before release — reading grip, arm slot, release point. That's anticipation, and it's why the best hitters describe the ball as "slow."

Exhibit 2 · The aging experiment

Velocity doesn’t beat reflexes. It beats anticipation.

Raw reaction speed peaks in your early 20s. The cue library — anticipation — only grows. So here's a natural experiment: if old hitters survive on prediction, they should hang with young hitters on ordinary fastballs and fall apart exactly where prediction stops working — top-end velocity, where the decision window collapses toward zero.

Exhibit 3 · The biological floor

Raw reaction hit a wall. Anticipation is the only door left.

World Athletics disqualifies any start under 100 ms on the grounds that no human can react that fast — it must have been a guess. The fastest humans alive, wired to force plates in the blocks, cluster at 0.11–0.18 s and haven't gotten faster in decades. Everything that separates elite from ordinary happens before the stimulus. (Noah Lyles had the slowest reaction in the Paris final. He won it.)

Sources & method

  • Pitch data: Baseball Savant / Statcast, 2023–24 regular seasons, four-seam fastballs. Flight time computed as (60.5 ft − release extension) ÷ average ball velocity, with the ball assumed to lose ~9.5% of release speed by the plate.
  • Visual latency (~100 ms to conscious visual processing) and bat swing execution (~150 ms) are standard sports-science figures used for illustration; exact values vary by player and study.
  • Whiff = swinging strike ÷ swings; hitter age = seasonal age (June 30). Sample sizes shown in tooltips and table view.
  • Reaction times: official World Athletics results — Paris 2024 final, Tokyo 2020 final. False-start rule: World Athletics TR16.7 (reaction < 100 ms).
  • ~273 ms median human click reaction: Human Benchmark aggregate data (visual stimulus + mouse click, so includes device latency; the Olympic figures are force-plate measurements — different instruments, same order of magnitude).
